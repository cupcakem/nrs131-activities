Breastfeeding Lecture 6 · In-Class Activity

Clinical Case Scenarios

Small group discussion · 4 vignettes with guided questions

Instructions: Divide into small groups (3–4 students). Each group reads the scenario, discusses the questions, and selects a spokesperson to share with the class. Do not reveal the answer key until group discussion is complete. NCLEX-style questions are marked NCLEX.
1
Scenario

"My milk hasn't come in yet" — The Worried New Dad

Setting: Postpartum floor, day 2. You are the nurse assigned to a first-time mother, Anika, 28 years old, who delivered vaginally 30 hours ago. Anika's mother-in-law is at the bedside and has told Anika's husband, Deshi, that "Anika has no milk yet" and that the baby "must be starving." Deshi is visibly anxious and is asking you to bring formula because the baby is feeding every 1.5–2 hours and seems fussy between feedings. Anika wants to breastfeed but is beginning to doubt herself.

Discussion Questions

1
Is the mother-in-law's concern clinically valid? What is the normal volume of colostrum on day 2, and how would you explain this to the family? NCLEX
2
The baby is feeding every 1.5–2 hours and appears fussy. Is this normal? How do you explain frequent feeding and cluster feeding to a worried family?
3
What signs would you assess to determine whether the baby is getting adequate intake? What findings would be reassuring?
4
How do you navigate the family dynamics here — respecting the grandmother's role while also supporting Anika's breastfeeding goal?

Answer Key — Instructor Reference

1

No, this is not a clinical concern. Colostrum volume of 2–20 mL per feeding is completely normal and physiologically appropriate on days 1–5. The stomach of a newborn is approximately the size of a marble. Colostrum is highly concentrated — rich in antibodies (IgA) and protein. Reassure the family with facts: small volume is by design, not a deficit. Mature milk will come in around days 3–5.

2

Yes, completely normal. Newborns should feed 8–12 times per 24 hours — approximately every 1.5–3 hours. Cluster feeding (frequent feeds close together, often evenings) is physiologic and helps establish supply through supply-and-demand signaling. Watching the clock creates anxiety; teach the family to watch the baby. Fussiness between feeds is common and doesn't mean inadequate intake.

3

Signs of adequate intake: wet diapers (1–2 day 1, increasing to 6+ by day 4–5), stool progression from meconium to transitional to mustard-yellow seedy by day 5, audible swallowing during feeds, baby appears content after feeding, weight loss within 7–10% of birth weight (normal), returns to birth weight by 10–14 days.

4

Use inclusive, respectful communication. Acknowledge the grandmother's concern and experience. Include the family in teaching — "Let me share what the latest research tells us so we can all support Anika." Frame breastfeeding support as a team effort. Avoid dismissing the grandmother, which can create conflict and undermine the mother's confidence further. If conflict persists, consider a private conversation with Anika to reinforce her autonomy and offer an IBCLC consult.

2
Scenario

"It hurts every time" — Latch and Nipple Pain

Setting: Postpartum, day 3. Maya, 34 years old, G2P2, is breastfeeding her second child. She breastfed her first child for 4 months before stopping due to pain. She reports that every feeding is painful throughout — not just at latch. You observe a feeding and notice the baby appears to have dimpled cheeks, is making clicking sounds, and Maya's nipple comes out creased and compressed after the baby releases.

Discussion Questions

1
Based on your observations, what is your nursing assessment? Is this normal postpartum nipple soreness or is something else going on? NCLEX
2
What specific latch findings are you seeing that indicate a poor latch? List at least 3 signs from this scenario.
3
What immediate nursing interventions would you implement? Walk through the steps of helping Maya re-latch.
4
Maya mentions her baby's frenulum looked short at birth — the pediatrician didn't say anything. How does this connect to your assessment, and what would you do? NCLEX

Answer Key — Instructor Reference

1

This is not normal nipple soreness. While mild initial latch discomfort is common in the first seconds, pain throughout the entire feeding indicates a poor latch. Signs like clicking, dimpled cheeks, and a creased, compressed nipple are all red flags. This pattern also mirrors her previous breastfeeding experience, which is significant history — the same issue may have led to early cessation before.

2

Signs of poor latch observed: (1) clicking/smacking sounds — air leakage indicates poor seal; (2) dimpled cheeks — baby is sucking but not transferring milk efficiently; (3) nipple comes out creased/compressed — only the nipple tip is in the mouth, not the areola; (4) persistent pain throughout feeding (not just at initial latch). Four documented signs of a shallow latch.

3

Interventions: Break the suction gently with a clean finger, then re-latch. Unwrap baby. Position mom comfortably. Guide Maya to support the breast and stroke baby's lip to elicit a wide-mouth gape. When mouth is wide open, bring the baby to the breast (not breast to baby), chin touching first, aiming nipple toward roof of mouth. Ensure tummy-to-tummy alignment. Look for flanged lips and audible swallows. Reinforce: it should not hurt after the first seconds.

4

Short frenulum (ankyloglossia/tongue-tie) restricts tongue mobility, preventing the baby from cupping the breast correctly — this directly causes shallow latch, clicking, poor milk transfer, and nipple pain. The nurse should document the concern, note the history of poor latch in child 1, and make a referral to a lactation consultant (IBCLC) and/or the pediatrician for frenulum assessment. This is not outside the nurse's scope to identify and escalate.

3
Scenario

Going Back to Work — The Pumping Plan

Setting: Postpartum day 2, discharge teaching. Sofia, 29 years old, delivered via cesarean section yesterday. She is breastfeeding and tells you she plans to return to full-time work at 6 weeks. She wants to breastfeed for at least 6 months but has no experience with pumping and is feeling overwhelmed. She asks: "Can I even keep my milk up if I go back to work? How do I store it? Can my husband give the baby a bottle?"

Discussion Questions

1
How would you reassure Sofia and outline a realistic plan for maintaining milk supply when returning to work?
2
Review the safe breast milk storage guidelines with Sofia. How long can expressed milk be stored at room temperature? In the refrigerator? In the freezer? NCLEX
3
Sofia asks about introducing a bottle. What teaching would you provide about timing and nipple confusion?
4
Sofia had a cesarean section. She's currently struggling with positioning. Which breastfeeding positions would you recommend and why?

Answer Key — Instructor Reference

1

Yes, breastfeeding while working is achievable. Milk supply is driven by supply and demand — pumping regularly at work (every 2–3 hours) mimics what the baby would do. Key tips: establish a strong supply before returning (breastfeed on demand for the first 6 weeks), pump or nurse before leaving for work, pump during breaks at work, nurse as soon as home. Federal law protects her right to pump breaks and a private space at work (Break Time for Nursing Mothers Act).

2

Storage guidelines (CDC): Room temperature (77°F or cooler) — up to 4 hours; Refrigerator — up to 4 days; Freezer — 6 months is best, up to 12 months is acceptable. Additional teaching: use breast milk storage bags or food-grade BPA-free containers; store in small amounts (2–4 oz) to minimize waste; label with date; do not store in refrigerator or freezer door; insulated cooler with ice packs works up to 24 hours for travel.

3

Bottle introduction timing: Most lactation resources recommend waiting until breastfeeding is well established — typically 3–4 weeks — before introducing a bottle to reduce risk of nipple preference (sometimes called "nipple confusion"). After 6 weeks is even safer. Teach slow-flow nipple selection, paced bottle feeding technique (hold bottle horizontally, let baby set the pace), and that the husband's participation in bottle feeding can be a positive bonding experience while protecting the breastfeeding relationship.

4

C-section positioning: Recommend football hold (baby tucked under the arm, weight off the abdomen) and side-lying position (both lying down facing each other, no weight on the incision). Avoid cradle hold initially as infant weight rests on the abdomen near the incision. Ensure adequate pillow support in all positions. Laid-back nursing may also be comfortable once pain is controlled.

4
Scenario

"I don't want to breastfeed" — Supporting the Formula-Feeding Mother

Setting: Postpartum floor, 6 hours after a vaginal delivery. Destiny, 24 years old, G1P1, tells you clearly and without hesitation that she does not want to breastfeed. She has decided to formula feed. Her nurse from the previous shift documented "patient refuses breastfeeding education" and did not provide formula feeding instruction. Destiny tells you she feels judged. Her breasts are beginning to feel full and uncomfortable.

Discussion Questions

1
How do you feel about the previous nurse's documentation and approach? What ethical principle is most relevant here? NCLEX
2
What teaching does Destiny need that she did not receive? Outline the key content for safe formula preparation and storage.
3
Destiny's breasts are becoming full and uncomfortable. What is happening physiologically, and what nursing interventions would you provide for lactation suppression?
4
How do you provide emotional support to a mother who feels judged for her infant feeding choice? What language and approach would you use? NCLEX

Answer Key — Instructor Reference

1

This was a nursing care failure. Declining to breastfeed is not the same as refusing education about infant feeding. The ethical principle of autonomy — the patient's right to make informed decisions — was violated by withholding relevant teaching. Every patient who feeds a baby has a right to accurate education about their chosen method. Documentation of "refuses education" is also inaccurate and potentially harmful to continuity of care.

2

Formula feeding teaching: All FDA-approved formulas are nutritionally equivalent — no brand is superior. Hand washing before preparation. Use clean, sanitized bottles. Formula does not need to be warmed — if warmed, use warm water (never microwave). Check temperature on wrist. Follow mixing directions exactly (powdered/concentrated). Do not dilute or over-concentrate. Discard prepared formula after 2 hours at room temperature or 1 hour once feeding starts. Opened containers: use within 1 month, store at room temperature (not refrigerator).

3

Physiologically, prolactin levels rise whether or not the mother breastfeeds — milk production is initiated by the drop in progesterone after placental delivery, not by the baby's demand. Without stimulation, milk production will decrease over several days. Lactation suppression measures: well-fitting (snug but not tight) supportive bra worn 24 hrs/day; avoid breast stimulation; cold compresses or ice packs; cold cabbage leaves (evidence-supported for comfort); mild analgesics (ibuprofen, acetaminophen); adequate fluid intake. Medications for suppression are not used (no longer recommended; serious side effects).

4

Therapeutic communication approach: Begin by acknowledging her experience directly — "I'm sorry you felt judged. Your decision is yours to make, and my job is to support you and your baby, not to pressure you." Use neutral, non-comparative language (avoid "breast is best" framing). Focus on what she needs: practical information about safe formula feeding and infant care. Validate that she is doing the right thing by asking questions and seeking information. The nurse's role is to empower, not evaluate her choice.
