Latch & Positioning

Getting a good latch, position options, and breast/nipple assessment

🎯 Steps to a Good Latch
1

Unwrap the baby

Remove swaddle so the baby can use their hands and feel skin-to-skin contact. A swaddled baby latches poorly.

2

Position mom comfortably

Mom must be relaxed with good back support. Arms supported with pillows to prevent fatigue.

3

Stroke the infant's lip

Gently stroke the lower lip with the nipple. Most babies will automatically open their mouth wide.

4

Wait for a WIDE open mouth

Do not force the latch until the mouth is wide open — like a yawn. A small mouth opening leads to a shallow, painful latch.

5

Ensure tongue is under the breast

The tongue should be cupped under the nipple and areola. A tongue-tied baby may not be able to do this effectively.

6

Bring baby close — tummy to tummy

Baby's entire body should face mom, tummy to tummy. Ear, shoulder, and hip in alignment. Baby comes to the breast, not breast to baby.

⚠

NO hands on the baby's head!

Never push on the occiput. Apply pressure on the upper back or base of skull only if needed. Pushing the head causes the baby to pull back and arch away.

7

If nipple needs help protruding

Have mom gently rub the nipple with her fingers to help it protrude before attempting latch. This is especially helpful with flat or inverted nipples.

✅ Good Latch vs. Poor Latch

✅ Signs of a Good Latch

  • Mouth wide open, lips flanged outward
  • Most of areola in the mouth
  • Chin touching breast
  • Rhythmic sucking with audible swallows
  • No pain (beyond initial latch)
  • Cheeks rounded, not dimpled

⚠️ Signs of a Poor Latch

  • Only nipple in mouth (not areola)
  • Clicking or smacking sounds
  • Dimpled cheeks
  • Persistent pain throughout feeding
  • Nipple comes out compressed or creased
  • Baby seems frustrated or falling off
🤱 Four Breastfeeding Positions
Cradle Hold

Infant's head rests on mother's forearm, tummy-to-tummy. The classic position — works best once latch is established.

Most common
Cross-Cradle Hold

Opposite hand supports the infant's head, allowing more control. Good for newborns and latch difficulty.

Great for newborns
Football Hold

Infant tucked under the arm like a football. Keeps baby's weight off abdomen — ideal after cesarean section.

✓ C-section friendly
Side-Lying Hold

Mom and baby lie facing each other. Allows mom to rest during night feedings. Also C-section friendly.

✓ C-section friendly
🔍 Nursing Assessment of Mom

Nipple Assessment

  • Flat nipples
  • Inverted nipples
  • Nipple injury / cracking
  • Sore vs. painful (expect initial soreness)

Breast Assessment

  • Prior surgeries: augmentation, reduction, mastectomy
  • Size not related to ability to breastfeed
  • Engorgement
  • Plugged milk duct
  • Signs of mastitis

Relevant History

  • Previous breastfeeding experience
  • Medical conditions (HIV, active TB)
  • Cultural beliefs and family support
  • Education level and support system

Laid-Back / Biological Nurturing

  • Mom reclines at ~45 degrees
  • Baby placed tummy-down on mom
  • Gravity helps baby stay latched
  • Instinctive for many newborns

✔ Knowledge Check


Football hold and side-lying position are both C-section friendly because they keep the baby's weight off the abdominal incision, reducing pain and discomfort for the mother.

Pushing on the occiput causes the baby to arch away and resist the breast. Pressure should be applied on the upper back if needed. This is a very common latch mistake.

Inverted nipples make initial latching more challenging. The nurse can teach the mother to gently roll the nipple between her fingers to promote protrusion before latching. Nipple shields may also be considered. Breastfeeding is still possible.

Any 3 of: wide open mouth with lips flanged out, most of the areola in the mouth, chin touching the breast, audible swallowing, no persistent pain, cheeks rounded not dimpled.
