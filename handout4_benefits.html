Benefits of Breastfeeding

Infant benefits, maternal benefits, and the nurse's role in support

Breastfeeding provides unmatched health benefits for babies and mothers. It is the clinical gold standard for infant feeding and nutrition, with breast milk uniquely tailored to meet the health needs of a growing baby.

— Dr. Ruth Petersen, Director, CDC Division of Nutrition, Physical Activity, and Obesity

🌍 Organizational Recommendations
American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)

Exclusive BF for ~6 Months

The AAP recommends exclusive breastfeeding for approximately 6 months, then continued breastfeeding with complementary foods for 2 years or beyond as mutually desired.

Updated 2022 statement
World Health Organization (WHO)

Consistent with AAP

WHO recommendations are consistent with AAP — exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months, continued with complementary foods up to 2 years or beyond.

Global standard
👩‍⚕️ The Nurse's Role in Breastfeeding Support

Supporting the Breastfeeding Dyad

Golden Hour
Facilitate uninterrupted skin-to-skin contact in the first hour after birth. Minimize unnecessary interventions. This is when the first latch most naturally occurs.
Anticipatory Guidance
Teach parents what to expect before it happens — small colostrum volumes, feeding frequency, engorgement, normal weight loss — so they don't panic or supplement unnecessarily.
Cultural Humility
Assess cultural beliefs, family support, and prior experiences. Never assume breastfeeding is the goal — explore the mother's wishes and support her informed decision.
Non-Judgmental Support
Some mothers cannot or choose not to breastfeed. The nurse's role is to provide accurate information and support the mother's well-being — not shame or pressure.
IBCLC Referral
Know when to refer to a lactation consultant (IBCLC). Complex latch issues, history of breast surgery, tongue-tie, premature infants, and persistent pain warrant specialist support.

✔ Knowledge Check


Exclusive breastfeeding for approximately 6 months, then continued breastfeeding with complementary foods for 2 years or beyond, as long as mutually desired by mother and child.

Infant (any 3): reduced asthma, obesity, Type 1 diabetes, lower respiratory disease, ear infections, SIDS, GI infections, NEC in preterm infants. Mother (any 2): reduced hypertension, Type 2 diabetes, ovarian cancer, breast cancer.

Respect the mother's decision. Provide non-judgmental support, ask if she would like information about formula feeding, and assist with lactation suppression as needed. The nurse's role is to support the mother's informed choice — not to pressure or shame her.

The Golden Hour is the first hour after birth, ideally with uninterrupted skin-to-skin contact between mother and infant. This is when the first latch most naturally and successfully occurs, driven by the newborn's instinctive behaviors during the quiet alert phase.
